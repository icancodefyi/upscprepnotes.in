1. Fundamental Rights — Key Articles & Judgments

Article 12 — Definition of State

Text: Defines "State" for Part III enforcement — includes Government and Parliament of India, Government and Legislature of States, all local authorities, and other authorities within India or under control of Government of India.
Landmark Judgment: Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002) — The Supreme Court evolved the "agency or instrumentality of State" test. Key factors: deep and pervasive state control, functional character (government functions), state's shareholding, monopoly status. The judgment overruled the narrow interpretation in Sabhajit Tewary (1975) and expanded the scope of Article 12.
Contemporary Relevance: Used to determine writ jurisdiction against PSUs, regulatory bodies, and even private entities performing public functions.

Article 13 — Laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights

Text: All laws in force before the Constitution and any future laws inconsistent with Part III shall be void to the extent of inconsistency. Clarifies that "law" includes ordinances, orders, by-laws, rules, regulations, notifications, and customs.
Landmark Judgment: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) — The basic structure doctrine emerged from this case. The Court held that Article 13 does not bar amending the Constitution under Article 368 but amendments that destroy the basic structure are void. This established judicial review of constitutional amendments.
Contemporary Relevance: The basic structure test continues to be applied to constitutional amendments (NJAC case, 2015).

Article 14 — Right to Equality

Text: The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or equal protection of the laws within India.
Landmark Judgment: E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974) — The Court introduced the "arbitrariness test" — Article 14 strikes at arbitrariness in state action. This was elaborated in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) where the Court held that Article 14, 19, and 21 are not mutually exclusive but form a "golden triangle."
Contemporary Relevance: Used to challenge discriminatory laws, arbitrary executive action, and as the foundation for the anti-arbitrariness principle in administrative law.

Article 15 — Prohibition of Discrimination

Text: Prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Allows special provisions for women, children, SCs, STs, and socially/educationally backward classes.
Landmark Judgment: Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) — Mandal case — Upheld 27% OBC reservation. Key holdings: Creamy layer must be excluded from OBC reservation; reservation in promotions is not constitutionally mandated; total reservation should not exceed 50%; no reservation in certain services (defence, judiciary).
Contemporary Relevance: Basis for ongoing debates on EWS reservation, Maratha reservation, OBC sub-categorisation.

Article 19 — Six Freedoms

Text: Protects six freedoms: (a) speech and expression, (b) peaceful assembly, (c) associations/unions, (d) movement throughout India, (e) residence and settlement, (f) profession/occupation/trade. All subject to reasonable restrictions.
Landmark Judgment: Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) — Struck down Section 66A of IT Act as vague and overbroad. The Court held that the "reasonable restrictions" under Article 19(2) require a direct and proximate nexus with the restriction and the grounds mentioned. The three-fold test for Article 19 restrictions: (i) restriction must be by law, (ii) grounds must be specified, (iii) restriction must be reasonable.
Contemporary Relevance: Basis for internet freedom cases, sedition law challenges, and media freedom issues.

Article 21 — Right to Life and Personal Liberty

Text: No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law.
Landmark Judgment: Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) — Transformed Article 21 from a narrow procedural right to a substantive fundamental right. The Court held that the "procedure established by law" must be "fair, just and reasonable." This judgment created the "golden triangle" of Articles 14, 19, and 21.
Contemporary Relevance: Source of numerous rights: right to privacy (Puttaswamy), right to clean environment, right to food, right to health, right to livelihood, right to die with dignity (Common Cause).

Article 21 — Right to Privacy

Text: Recognised as a facet of Article 21 through judicial interpretation.
Landmark Judgment: Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) — 9-judge bench — Unanimously declared right to privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21. Privacy includes: (i) decisional autonomy (personal choices), (ii) informational privacy (data protection), (iii) spatial privacy (bodily integrity). The three-fold test for privacy infringement: legality, legitimate aim, proportionality.
Contemporary Relevance: Basis for Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023; used to challenge Aadhaar, surveillance, CAA-NPR-NRC.

Article 25 — Freedom of Religion

Text: Freedom of conscience and free profession, practice, and propagation of religion, subject to public order, morality, and health.
Landmark Judgment: S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) — While primarily about Article 356, the judgment held that secularism is part of the basic structure of the Constitution. The state must maintain equal distance from all religions. Religious practices can be regulated for social reform and public order.
Contemporary Relevance: Basis for temple entry, religious conversion debates, personal law reforms.

Article 32 — Right to Constitutional Remedies

Text: Right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. The SC can issue writs — habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, certiorari. Dr. Ambedkar called it the "heart and soul of the Constitution."
Landmark Judgment: L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997) — Held that the power of judicial review under Articles 32 and 226 is part of the basic structure. Even tribunals established under Article 323-A and 323-B cannot exclude the jurisdiction of High Courts and Supreme Court.
Contemporary Relevance: Article 32 can be suspended only during a national emergency (Article 359). The increasing pendency of writ petitions raises questions about access to justice.

FR-DPSP Relationship — Evolution through Jurisprudence

The relationship between Fundamental Rights (Part III) and Directive Principles (Part IV) has evolved significantly:

  • Champakam Dorairajan (1951): FRs prevail over DPSPs — DPSPs are subordinate to FRs. This led to the First Amendment.
  • Golaknath (1967): FRs cannot be amended even to implement DPSPs.
  • Kesavananda Bharati (1973): FRs can be amended but basic structure cannot be destroyed.
  • Minerva Mills (1980): Harmony between FR and DPSP is part of basic structure. Neither can be sacrificed for the other.
  • Unnikrishnan (1993): DPSPs can be read into FRs to expand their scope (right to education as FR under Art 21).
GS2 Tip: When answering questions on welfare schemes, social justice, or education policy, always frame the response as "DPSP implementation through legislative/executive action." The DPSP + Fundamental Rights + Directive Principles framework shows a holistic constitutional understanding.

Quick Reference: Three Houses of Indian Parliament
Lok Sabha: 543 elected + 2 nominated Anglo-Indian (up to 2020 by 104th Amendment) = 545. Full term: 5 years. Presiding Officer: Speaker.
Rajya Sabha: 233 elected + 12 nominated = 245. Permanent House — 1/3 retire every 2 years. Presiding Officer: Chairman (Vice President).
State Assemblies: Maximum 500, minimum 60 (exceptions exist). Vidhan Parishad (Council) in 6 states only.

Common Error: Students often confuse writ jurisdiction under Article 32 (SC) and Article 226 (HC). Remember: Article 32 is available ONLY for Fundamental Rights, and SC's jurisdiction is limited. Article 226 is wider — it covers any legal right and can be issued against private persons if they perform public functions. Article 226 is the workhorse for most litigation.

Punchhi Commission Recommendations on Federalism

The Punchhi Commission (2007-10) on Centre-State Relations made the following key recommendations to strengthen federalism:

  • Governor: Appointment through a committee of PM, CM, and CJI; removal only by impeachment-like process; discretionary powers should be limited; should not be a political appointee.
  • Article 356: Should be used only as a last resort; President's rule proclamation must be approved by Parliament within 1 month; state assembly should not be dissolved until proclamation is approved.
  • Inter-State Council: Must meet at least thrice a year; establish a separate secretariat; should be the primary forum for Centre-State coordination.
  • Financial: GST compensation mechanism; Finance Commission's role to be strengthened; state borrowing limits to be made more flexible.
  • Residuary Powers: Re-examination of Article 248 which gives residuary powers to Centre; some areas should be shared.
  • Concurrent List: Remove certain subjects from Concurrent List and transfer to State List.
Federalism Answer Strategy: When answering GS2 questions on Centre-State relations, use the "cooperative federalism" framing (not unitary vs federal binary). Reference: (1) GST Council as cooperative federalism example, (2) Punchhi recommendations for reform, (3) Supreme Court's SR Bommai judgment on federalism as basic structure, (4) Finance Commission awards for fiscal federalism.

Landmark Judgments on Emergency

ADM Jabalpur v. Shivakant Shukla (1976) — Habeas Corpus case: The Supreme Court held that the right to approach courts under Article 226 (habeas corpus) is suspended during emergency. This was heavily criticised. Justice H.R. Khanna dissented — "even in the darkest hour, the law does not permit the state to take away the right to life." The 44th Amendment (1978) reversed this by providing that Article 20 (protection against ex post facto law, double jeopardy, self-incrimination) and Article 21 (right to life) cannot be suspended even during emergency.

S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994): Established judicial review of President's Rule under Article 356. Key holdings: (i) President's satisfaction under Article 356 is justiciable, (ii) secularism is part of basic structure and its violation justifies Article 356, (iii) state assembly should not be dissolved until proclamation is approved by Parliament, (iv) Article 356 cannot be used to settle political scores.

Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006): Imposition of President's Rule in Bihar before the formation of government was malafide. The court held that the Governor's report recommending Article 356 can be scrutinised by courts for mala fides, non-application of mind, or arbitrariness.

44th Amendment Act 1978 — Key Safeguards: Article 21 cannot be suspended; Article 19 can be suspended only during war/aggression, not armed rebellion; President must receive written Cabinet advice; Parliamentary approval required every month; internal emergency requires "armed rebellion" not "internal disturbance" (narrower ground).

7. Amendment Process & Basic Structure Doctrine

Article 368 — Amendment Process

Three Types:

  • Simple Majority: Articles 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 10, 11, 75(5), 101, 102, 105, 108, 124(1), 135, 144, 169, 171, 180, 190, 197, 202, 214, 240, 299, 306, 310, 311, 343-345, 348, 349, 350, 371 — outside the scope of Article 368.
  • Special Majority (2/3 of present and voting + majority of total membership of each House): Most amendments — rights, DPSP, structure of institutions, etc.
  • Special Majority + Ratification by 50% State Legislatures: Matters affecting federal structure — election of President (54, 55), extent of executive power (73, 162), High Courts (214-231), Supreme Court (124-147), distribution of legislative powers (245-255), representation of states in Parliament (80-81), Article 368 itself.

Elements of Basic Structure (evolved over time)

  • Supremacy of the Constitution
  • Republican and Democratic form of Government
  • Secular character of the Constitution
  • Separation of powers
  • Federal character of the Constitution
  • Unity and integrity of the nation
  • Judicial review (power of courts to review laws)
  • Rule of law
  • Independence of judiciary
  • Free and fair elections
  • Balance between Fundamental Rights and DPSP
  • Equality of status and opportunity
  • Dignity of the individual (Article 21's core)
  • Welfare state (social justice as core)
  • Sovereign, Democratic, Republican structure
Common Mud Point: The basic structure doctrine applies ONLY to constitutional amendments, not to ordinary legislation. Ordinary laws are challenged on the ground that they violate Fundamental Rights (Article 13), not that they violate basic structure. This distinction is crucial for GS2 answers.

Final Strategy — The Judgment-Article Link: For every GS2 answer, follow this formula: (1) Identify relevant constitutional article(s), (2) Cite a landmark judgment interpreting that article, (3) State the contemporary relevance or application. Example: "Article 21, as interpreted in Puttaswamy (2017), includes the right to privacy, which is now regulated by the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023." This three-step formula works for any constitutional question.
